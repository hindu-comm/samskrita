> Several Sanskrit words like मुख mean both mouth and face and the > context makes it clear. Vyutpatti wise "aasya" (place of > pronunciation) and "vadana" (वदति अनेन) primarily mean mouth and > secondarily face.  
>   
> There are some words for mouth which do not mean the face -  
>   
> 1. आललम् - अलामक्षराणां समूहः आलं तं लाति वक्ति इति आललम् - > Sribhargavaraghaviyam 20/94  
> 2. ग्रसनम् - ग्रसति खादति अनेन - Bhagavata 3/13/36  
> 3. लपनम् - लपति वक्ति अनेन - Shishupalavadha 7/10  
> 4. सृक्वम् - means corner of the mouth  
>   
> As mentioned, when a suffix is added like मुखमण्डल or वदनेन्दु or > मुखकमल - the meaning is face and not the mouth. >

करारविन्देन*पदारविन्दं मुखारविन्दे*विनिवेशयन्तम् ।

वटस्य पत्रस्य पुटे शयानं बालं मुकुन्दं मनसा स्मरामि ॥



Here it is clear that it is not face, but mouth only. Not always, but many a times, the context decides the meaning. The following are the considerations for clearing the ambiguity :

  

संयोगो विप्रयोगश्चसाहचर्यं विरोधिता।  
अर्थः प्रकरणं लिङ्गं शब्दस्यान्यस्य सन्निधिः।।  
सामर्थ्यमौचिती देशः कालो व्यक्तिः स्वरादयः।  
शब्दार्थस्यानवच्छेदे विशेषस्मृतिहेतवः।।  

  

as listed by Bhartrihari, in वाक्यपदीय. That means not always the vytpatti-wise analysis will help much. Only in certain cases, where there is no transference of meaning, and the meaning conveyed is directly based on the literal meaning of the verb, it may be helpful.

> > > > The Indian mathematician Mahāvīra (9th century CE) gave the formula > > for nCr, the number of ways to select ‘r’ entities out of ‘n’. The > > formula was known to Indian mathematicians much before. In the > > Bṛhatsaṃhitā (6th century CE), verse 76.20, Varāhamihira states the > > following, in an āryā metre, while discussing how to make perfumes— > > > > षोडशके द्रव्यगणे चतुर्विकल्पेन भिद्यमानानाम्।  
> > अष्टादश जायन्ते शतानि सहितानि विंशत्या॥  
> > ṣoḍaśake dravyagaṇe caturvikalpena bhidyamānānām  
> > aṣṭādaśa jāyante śatāni sahitāni viṃśatyā > > > >  > style="display:inline;font-family:Helvetica,Arial,sans-serif;color:rgb(28,30,33);font-size:14px"> > > > > Translation by V. Subramanya Sastri—“Out of a collection of sixteen > > kinds of substances, the number of perfumes that can be made by > > selecting any four will be 1820.” > > > > My translation—“When there is a collection of sixteen entities, on > > being distinguished by options (=selections) of four, there arise > > eighteen hundreds along with twenty (18 × 100 + 20 = 1,820).” > > > > What Varāhamihira says that 16C4 = 1,820, which is correct as 16C4 = > > (16 × 15 × 14 × 13) / (1 × 2 × 3 × 4) = 1,820. Surely Varāhamihira > > knew the formula for nCr. > > > >
